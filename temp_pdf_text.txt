Abstract—The problem for current RAG systems lies in the
fundamental trade - off of exact recall for keyword systems
(sparse retrieval) versus recall based on a sentence’s seman-
tics/meaning but without exact keyword recall (dense retrieval).
Hybrid systems also poorly exploit strong rerankers by first stage,
subpar sparse or dense systems. Therefore, this project proposes a
training - free, 3-stage hybrid pipeline, which involves first - stage
BM25 retrieval, first - stage dense retrieval (using BGE - large -
en - v1.5 with PyTorch chunked exact search), followed by first
- stage reranking and a third stage of second - stage reranking
based on token - level interaction using ColBERTv2, all fused
with Reciprocal Rank Fusion. The RAG pipeline achieved 0.4638
nDCG@10 and 0.3825 MRR@10 on MS MARCO. Standalone
BM25 achieves nDCG@10 = 0.2286 and an exact dense retrieval
pipeline achieves nDCG@10 = 0.4376. Zero - shot performance
across 4 BEIR datasets shows TriRank generalizes well without
fine - tuning, providing high precision while also being able to
be deployed off - the - shelf and as a scalable alternative.
Index Terms—Information Retrieval, Hybrid Search, BGE Em-
beddings, ColBERTv2, Reciprocal Rank Fusion, Dense Retrieval
I. INTRODUCTION
Modern AI-based systems increasingly depend on Retrieval
Augmented Generation (RAG) [1] to power data retrieval
for Large Language Models (LLMs). These models, through
RAG, should, in theory, be able to answer questions grounded
in external knowledge bases. Yet in practice, these pipelines
fail regularly at fetching or retrieving accurate information.
Enterprise search systems - which power their RAG pipelines,
often fail at retrieving accurate and grounded information
from their usually vast and well-organised databases. These
issues arise mainly because of inaccurate keyword matching,
poor semantic search querying, or inadequate indexing
strategies.
This failure usually propagates downstream and is passed
to the LLM, which confidently generates hallucinated
answers based on the inaccurate retrievals. This leads to
factual inaccuracies, hallucinations, Shallow and unverified
responses, these issues may evolve from being merely
inconvenient to outright consequential in domains like
Finance, Healthcare, or legal research. This gap between
available knowledge and effective retrieval is one of the
major bottlenecks in modern, enterprise-grade retrieval and
generation systems.
Prior retrieval systems utilize a hybrid architecture that
combines sparse methods such as BM25 [9] with average
dense retrievers such as Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR) [6] or
MiniLM [13], or they rely on learned sparse representations
such as SPLADE [8]. Other methods like using a late interac-
tion architecture such as ColBERTv2 [3] have been explored
as rerankers on top of various first-stage retrievers - their
performance has not been evaluated when paired with state-
of-the-art embedding models that achieve top rankings when
evaluated against the MTEB benchmarks [10]. Meanwhile,
the new and leading embedding models like BGE-large-en-
v1.5 [11] have only been tested in isolation and not integrated
into multi-stage pipelines. This, therefore, represents a critical
research gap, as the effectiveness of re-ranking depends on the
first layer of the first retrieval; a better quality first layer could
significantly improve the upper bound for the performance
achieved by the second layers.
The main contributions of this paper are as follows.
(1) TriRank, A hybrid retrieval pipeline that has three stages.
It integrates BM25 sparse retrieval [9], BGE-large-en-
v1.5 dense retrieval [11] via chunked exact search, and
ColBERTv2 [3] token-level re-ranking fused through
Reciprocal Rank Fusion [7], the first system to combine
a top-ranked open source Embedding model with a late
interaction reranking.
(2) Through evaluation on MS MARCO [4] and four BEIR
[5] datasets, we demonstrate that combining high-quality
open-source embedding models along with token-level
reranking consistently outperforms other isolated retrievalParallel Retrieval Stage

II. RELATEDWORK
The field of search has moved on since the days of TF -
IDF/BM25 [?], with papers proposing, for example, dense
models or Hybrid retrieval approaches that combine dense
and sparse. A couple of years ago (e. g. MonoT5 [14],
monoELECTRA) introduced cross - encoder rerankers that,
by considering interactions between a specific query and
a specific document, improved upon BM25 by far (in the
relevant work to MonoT5: <0.05> vs BM25 <0.19> difference
on the TREC DL MS MARCO track). Since cross - encoders
require computing dense representations for both query and
document, the search index and search time grows , making
it impossible to scale search with it to many thousands of
users/queries. Thus, last year, a variety of papers proposed
late interaction models such as ColBERTv2 [3] that, after
independently encoding query tokens and document tokens,
perform efficient token - level matching, giving a better trade
- off between performance and accuracy.
To boost even further performance, we combine retrieval
augmented generation (RAG) methods from Retrieval -
Augmented Generation [9] with late interaction methods and
an improved first - stage retriever. In many cases, single -
stage retrievers like BM25 [?] or one of several dense retrieval
approaches are used in the first stage of a RAG system.
However, this means that a significant part of the relevant
context is lost in the first stage and we can’t improve the
second stage (e. g. Using reranking or cross - encoders) since
we’d receive far too many incorrect or irrelevant contexts.
Thus, we present an approach that boosts the first - stage
retrieval.
In terms of how embedding retrieval performance can be
measured, there are benchmark suites such as MTEB [10] that
measure how well an embedding model performs over several
retrieval tasks (including MS MARCO, BEIR, GloVe and
more). For this reason, high aggregate scores don’t necessarily
mean good retrieval on all tasks. One popular dataset is MS
MARCO [4], but it also contains noise. Another dataset used
by some embedding benchmarks is BEIR [5], a collection of
retrieval datasets that vary by genre, noise and domain. The
embedding model BGE - large - en - v1.5 [11] scored well
on these benchmarks, but we need to confirm these claims
for actual retrieval setups. Hybrid retrieval isn’t a new idea.
Already two years ago, people demonstrated the effectiveness
of DPR+BM25 [6] retrieval methods, whereas models such
as SPLADE [8] and DRAGON ignored the lexical signal
(SPLADE), omitted reranking (SPLADE, DRAGON) or com-
bined poor signals (DPR+BM25). More recently, with the ad-
vance in models like ColBERTv2 [3], people started building
powerful, task - agnostic, state - of - the - art search pipelines
combining lexical retrieval, dense embeddings, multiple fusion
methods such as Reciprocal Rank Fusion [7] and reranking
with late interaction components such as ColBERTv2 [3].

The output of sparse and dense retrievers is fused with
Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) [7].
During inference, a user query is simultaneously evaluated
by the BM25 retriever and the dense retriever to ensure low
latency. Each retriever returns its respective top-k candidate
passages, which are then combined via RRF to form the
candidate set. Finally, ColBERTv2 is employed to rerank the
combined candidates, leveraging token-level late interaction to
score relevance and generate the ultimate ranked results.
Different from hybrid systems that rely on task-specific
training or score normalization, TriRank utilizes exclusively
pretrained components and requires zero additional tuning or
modification, making it readily deployable and reproducible
whilst achieving potent retrieval effectiveness due to the com-
plementarity of its constituent parts.
A. Stage 1: Parallel Retrieval
Figure 1 shows the TriRank pipeline. The query first hits
two retrievers in parallel, BM25 [?] (sparse retrieval) and
BGE - large - en - v1.5 [11] (dense retrieval), with dense
vectors evaluated via exact search. In parallel, these systems
retrieve the top 50 passages, which are then combined and
reranked via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) [7]. The goal of
combining these two streams and using RRF is to get the
most important passages at the top, even when the systems’
importance scores diverge. This approach generates between
roughly 50-100 candidate passages per query and sends them
to a ColBERTv2 [3] reranker for scoring and filtering for the
final top 10.
B. Stage 2: Parallel Sparse and Dense Retrieval
When you retrieve passages with TriRank, we run two
retrieval systems at the same time and combine the final
rankings of the passages retrieved. With both methods, we
aim to take advantage of the complementary information in a
lexical retriever for broad coverage of documents and a dense
retriever that understands meaning and can achieve shorter
retrieval times by reducing noise and latency. First, we include
a sparse retriever built with Pyserini [2], where an inverted
index is built on the entire dataset of passages in the corpus,
and we then use the BM25 ranking algorithm [9] to calculate
scores for passages.

To bypass the memory bottlenecks of traditional vector
databases, document vectors are evaluated using a PyTorch
chunked exact search mechanism. This approach processes
the 8.8 million dense document embeddings in bounded
chunks, strictly constraining VRAM usage while computing
exact cosine similarities. This guarantees 100% recall of the
true top-knearest neighbors without the indexing overhead
or approximation errors associated with approximate nearest
neighbor (ANN) methods [12].

of the 100 or so candidates passed in each fused query and not
the full corpus. This reduction drastically cuts computational
costs compared to full reranking.
E. Stage 5: Computational Complexity and Scalability
The computational complexity of TriRank primarily consists
of that of each stage in its pipeline. BM25 [?] sparse retrieval
operates on top of an inverted index and scales roughly
with a logarithmic complexity of the corpus size,O(logN).
Likewise, the PyTorch chunked exact search computes exact
cosine similarities over the entire corpus. While naive exact
search scales linearly,O(N×d), our chunked implementation
leverages highly optimized GPU matrix multiplications [15] to
maintain practical retrieval latencies without approximation.
The RRF [7] fusion of the top 100 BM25 results and top
100 DenseNet results merges and re-sorts these results using
a linear operation in the number of elements fused, which
meansO(100 + 100) =O(k). The ColBERTv2 [3] reranking
stage has the following complexity:

F . Design Rationale
BM25 [?] is excellent for precise term matching, while
BGE - large - en - v1.5 [11] is great for retrieving documents
semantically similar to the query, but it may include many
irrelevant ones. BM25 and BGE are fused with reciprocal rank
fusion (RRF) [7], then ColBERTv2 [3] provides reranking of
the resulting set of candidate documents.
An important caveat to remember is that reranking of results
is contingent upon their inclusion in the original retrieval
candidate set; if an excellent search hit is missed at retrieval
time, later steps can’t add it to the top.
V. EXPERIMENTS ANDRESULTS
A. Experimental Setup and Baselines
To establish a robust baseline for our pipeline, we first
evaluated a sparse lexical retriever using the BM25 [?] al-
gorithm on the MS MARCO [4] passage ranking dataset. A
major challenge in scaling lexical retrieval is the memory
overhead of tokenizing massive corpora. To prevent out-of-
memory (OOM) failures when tokenizing the 8.8 million
passage corpus, we utilizedbm25s, a highly efficient, Scipy-
sparse memory-optimized implementation.
Our optimized BM25 baseline successfully indexed the
entire corpus and retrieved the top 100 documents for 6,980
validation queries. This standalone sparse baseline achieved
an nDCG@10 of 0.2286, an MRR@10 of 0.1796, and a
Recall@100 of 0.6335, providing a strong lexical foundation
for the subsequent dense retrieval and reranking stages.
For the dense retrieval baseline, we employed the BGE-
large-en-v1.5 [11] model. To bypass the memory bottlenecksTABLE II

excellent dense semantic retrieval and careful token - level

D. Ablation Studies
We perform ablation studies to provide evidence for our
choices. First, we analyze the size of the pool of candidates
passed from RRF [7] fusion to the ColBERTv2 [3] reranker.
We observe that a pool size of 50 is the best tradeoff with
nDCG@10 0.4646 and an average latency of 15.37 ms/query.
Pool sizes larger than 50, e. g. , 100 or 200, show a degradation
in top-10 accuracy due to the injection of noise and also a
drastic increase in computational overhead.
Second, we examine the RRF [7] smoothing parameter
k. We note that the intermediate nDCG before ColBERT
reranking peaks withk= 10(0.3784), as opposed to the
original choice ofk= 60(0.3547).
Lastly, we run the sparse and dense retrieval pipelines in
parallel using threads and obtain an average time savings
of roughly 60 ms/query, reducing the bottleneck prior to
reranking.
VI. DISCUSSION
The results show that performance increases of hybrid
methods stem primarily from complementarity between search
components. BM25 [?] has strong precision when there’s an
exact match and prevents failures for searches with infrequent
items or constrained keywords. Conversely, BGE - large - en
- v1.5 [11] captures general intent when vocabulary mismatch
stops the dense index from picking up the true concept.
Reciprocal Rank Fusion [7] helps connect these two compo-
nents. Its similarity scoring means there’s a penalty on density
retriever’s best performance, but RRF gives more diversity
in candidate results. The density retriever’s cross - attention
based method means the choice and quality of candidates
impacts results - if dense retrievers don’t bring it into the
top 10, there’s not much the last step can do. The most
obvious pitfall compared to single - stage dense retrievers
is the added computation, though sparse and dense retrievals
occur simultaneously to keep latency down.
However, ColBERT [3] MaxSim remains the bottleneck.
There’s a direct tradeoff between how large the candidate
pool is and how responsive the system remains. We found
that 50 is the highest size while staying near peak nDCG@10
performance for production use.
VII. CONCLUSION
TriRank is a scalable three - stage retrieval pipeline that
combines coarse lexical retrieval, accurate dense retrieval and
late token - level interaction reranking. By using memory
- optimized, parallel implementations, the pipeline scales to
trillions of documents such as MS MARCO [4] without hitting
common hardware limitations. The three - stage architecture
sets the upper bound on retrieval efficiency by returning a
score of 0.4638 nDCG@10 in MS MARCO and showing
strong performance in zero - shot retrieval across many BEIR
[5] domains. Future work includes investigating a learnable
routing strategy to dynamically skip the token - level reranker
(ColBERT [3]) when possible, thus further reducing the com-
